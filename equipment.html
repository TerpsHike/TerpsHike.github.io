Equipment

What you need to stay comfortable and in control.

Gear

Whether you’re doing a five-mile loop near your house or spending a week on the side of a mountain, having the right gear for a trip will keep you safe and comfortable.

Footwear

Generally, most of the outdoor activities you’ll participate in will require some sort of hiking boot. It can be difficult to pick out a shoe that suits your needs, so having a good understanding of hiking boot composition is important. We’ll go over the upper, the midsole, and the outsole. A boot’s “upper” is the part of the boot that extends past the ankle. These vary greatly in size and material, with a short upper being better for day hiking and a higher one meant for long backpacking trips with heavier weights. Full leather uppers are extremely tough and durable, making them best for rough terrain. Split leather/nylon mixes are less tough, but are more breathable; a good option if you are hiking on hot days in muggy weather. If you are planning on hiking in wet climates, a waterproof-membrane upper sacrifices durability for air-tightness. As a Maryland hiker, we recommend a split leather upper, as it's the most flexible option and ideal for a novice. The midsole of a hiking boot is the main body of the shoe, and supports the foot and ankle. They vary mostly on material and stiffness. The most common midsole material is EVA (ethylene vinyl acetate), which is inexpensive and makes for a less-firm midsole. While it can be layered for a stiffer boot, it's better to go for polyurethane if that's what you’re after. This is the more expensive material, and is usually best for intense hiking. We recommend focusing more on the feet of the boot than on the material. Unlike other components, the midsole will not adjust much to your foot, so it's a good idea to make sure it's comfortable when you buy it. The final important element of a hiking boot is the the outsole. This is the material that actually separates you from the ground, so we recommend always getting a rubber one. No other material is really acceptable or widely used. The biggest decision point on the outsole is firm versus soft. A soft outsole will give you excellent traction and grip, but it is generally less durable and will wear faster. A firm sole, often rubber mixed with a little carbon, is extremely durable and will last you longer, but may fare worse on slippery surfaces. This is a matter of personal preference, but in Maryland you’ll probably find that the hard sole is a better choice. A final thing to remember is that most hiking boots are made mostly of leather. This is great for long term durability and support, but it means that it needs time to form to the shape of your foot. Make sure you wear your new boots around before you hike in them, or you’re going to have painful feet!

Foods to bring

For a short hiking trip, it is usually better to consider carrying lighter foods that are still able to provide you with enough energy for the hike. Even if you do not think you will get hungry on your hike, it is best to carry some food as the additional exercise may make you hungrier than usual. Non-perishable foods are always your best bet, as they will be easier to keep fresh. Some of the easiest foods to bring for a short one-day hiking trip are:

  • Trail mix (Avoid chocolate mixes)
  • Dried fruit
  • Energy bars
  • Beef jerky
  • Peanut butter and jelly sandwiches

If you decide you want to go on a longer hiking trip over the weekend, the food you take will need to have a balance of proteins, carbohydrates, and fats. If your body does not receive the appropriate nutrients, your metabolic system will not perform as well. Your body will be strained from the lack of a proper diet, and you will be weakened making it more difficult to finish your hike and avoid cramps and soreness. In order to make your hiking trip more enjoyable, eating what your body craves is important. Some easy multi-day camping foods are listed below:

  • Bread (You can substitute tortillas for ease of packing)
  • Bananas
  • Apples
  • Peanut butter
  • Breakfast cereal
  • Crackers
  • Squeezable apple sauce
  • Poultry or fish pouches
  • Canned tuna
  • Freeze-dried meals

The items listed above are just a few of the many options available to you when you go hiking for a weekend. Bring a few of the snacks listed for taking a one-day hike refuel energy in the middle of a hike. Food preparation is an important step to having an enjoyable hiking experience for you and your friends.
