Bird Watching

When you go hiking, wildlife surrounds you whether you notice it or not. An increased awareness while hiking can give you a glimpse into the world of flora and fauna that makes hiking even more enjoyable. Some of the common animals on hiking trails near the University of Maryland are a variety of birds, deer, squirrels, groundhogs and rabbits. If you are interested in wildlife, UMD has a Wildlife Society open to students aspiring to have a major or a career in wildlife sciences.

If you would like to birdwatch on your hike, there are plenty of ways to get started. Birds are a common sight on campus, but if you enter more wooded areas while hiking, you will see a wider variety of birds. The easiest birds to sight as a beginner in this area are American Robins, Blue Jays, Baltimore Orioles, and Northern Cardinals because they all have distinctive coloring patterns that are recognizable.

If you are interested in figuring out a mystery bird you spot on your hike, there are many apps available for free. The Audubon Birds App gives you the ability to search through birds, first by their type, then by similarly shaped birds, and finally through images of different birds. For example, if you see want to find the American Robin within the app, you would first select perching birds, then the most similar picture, thrushes, then you would see the picture of the American Robin in the list of thrushes.

The app also has the ability to search for recent bird sightings near College Park, so the list of birds you need to look through may be shorter if someone else has observed that bird locally.

Terrain

Within Maryland, there are vastly different types of environments to hike ranging from sandy beaches on the eastern seaboard to the mountainous western regions of Maryland. College Park is situated between these two extremes in an area known as the Piedmont region. The Piedmont is characterized by valleys and rolling hills leading up to the foothills of the Appalachian Mountains. Maryland offers a variety of different terrain to hike depending on how far you want to travel to get to your hiking location. With each type of terrain, there are different considerations that need to be taken in order to have a safe and enjoyable hiking experience.

Piedmont

The greatest challenge in the Piedmont region is the variety of terrain difficulty. The trails within the Piedmont can range from steep inclines to relatively flat lands. In order to prepare for the hike you want to take, it is important that you research the trail and search for reviews to get an understanding of the difficulty before you start. In Maryland, these trails tend to be closest to population centers, and are therefore better maintained, however they will possibly be more crowded.

Mountains

The mountainous areas of western Maryland have steeper inclines than the Piedmont region, and the incline can tire hikers out very easily. Trails in this region are generally unimproved and narrow, so be sure to follow the trail blazes and watch your step! There is also a larger prevalence of rock scrambles and jagged rocks, so proper footwear may be required for ankle support. If the incline is very steep, you can zigzag to decrease the gradient. Following switchbacks also preserves the trail and is safer than running straight down the hill, as tempting as it may be after working so hard going up!

Coastal Plain

Hiking at the beach introduces new challenges that are different than hiking anywhere else. Hiking along a beach may seem easy at first glance, but the constantly uneven and slippery sand stresses your legs more easily than hard, flat surfaces. Trails away from the waterfront will generally be hard-packed soil with few rocks or obstacles, except for the occasional tree root. Close proximity to the water introduces more insect life, so you should be sure to pack insect repellant to avoid unnecessary pain. The heat and the humidity at the beach can be dangerous to unprepared hikers. In order to fight the sun’s beams bring sunscreen, a hat, sunglasses, and extra drinking water.

Seasons

No matter what season you choose to go hiking, the weather and temperature are important to consider when planning a hike. Check the weather forecast leading up to your hike to avoid inclement weather situations and extreme temperatures. Depending on the season, you can enjoy a variety of different scenery: fall colors, snow-covered trees, and blossoming flowers. Each season bring hikers new experiences to enjoy.

Fall

As summer draws to a close and fall begins, the temperatures start to become more mild and humidity levels begin to drop. As the temperatures begin to decrease, the bright red, oranges, and yellows of fall foliage transform forested trails for hikers to enjoy. Another benefit of hiking during the fall is the reduction of mosquitoes, biting flies, and other insects from colder temperatures. As the fall weather continues to change, the highs and lows throughout the day can vary greatly. To prepare for the changing temperatures, pack extra layers in case the temperatures drop as the day progresses toward evening. Another downside of the changing weather are the cold rains that can catch hikers unprepared as fall continues. In order to avoid getting drenched in a rainstorm or thunderstorm, or make sure you bring appropriate raingear, make sure to check the weather forecast leading up to your hike.

Winter

Spring semester begins with winter where we’re all wishing for snow days to get out of class. Along with the promise of sleeping in, winter bring incredible views of snow covered trees and frozen rivers that you can see during a winter hike. December, January, and February are the coldest months of the winter in College park with average lows below freezing. Storms during winter bring snow or cold rain, both of which are dangerous because they can rapidly lower your body temperature. Long periods outside in cold temperatures will also lower your body temperature. With a low body temperature, it is more likely that you get hypothermia or frostbite, so you must make sure to wear appropriate clothing and boots that will keep your body warm. Some key winter accessories to wear hiking are winter coats, gloves, hats, thick socks, and winter boots. If you are walking on snow, it is important that the boots you wear are waterproof to prevent frostbite on your toes. In addition to proper clothing, eating extra food prevents hypothermia because it provides your body with more energy to produce heat. If your fingers, toes, or other appendages start to feel numb, immediately make sure they are dry and add more layers to add warmth.

Spring

Spring is beginning and that means new life! Flowers start popping out of the ground and buds appear on trees. With new life comes a lot of rain, which means the majority of hiking in the spring is going to be very muddy. Be aware of cold spells; be sure to pack extra layers if it is going to be cold during your hike.Waterproof clothing and packs are important for this season to keep yourself and your supplies dry in case of rain.

Summer

The first few weeks of fall semester and the last few weeks of spring semester are the hottest days most of us experience on campus, unless you are staying over the summer. With extreme heat and humidity, hikers must be extra cautious to stay hydrated. Hikers should also be aware of air quality alerts that indicate elevated levels of pollution that may be unhealthy for those with sensitive respiratory systems and popup thunderstorms that can break branches and cause flash flooding along the trail. That being said, summer offers longer days that make an evening hike possible without racing to beat sunset.
