Love the Trail You’re On

“Leave no trace” — a philosophy popularized by the National Park Service — is a message to those who enjoy the outdoors. The environment deserves to be preserved for all, meaning that when you visit a location, it is your duty to leave things as they were when you arrived. The Leave No Trace Organization extends this philosophy into seven principles:

  • 1.) Plan Ahead and Prepare
  • 2.) Travel and Camp on Durable Surfaces
  • 3.) Dispose of Waste Properly
  • 4.) Leave What You Find
  • 5.) Minimize Campfire Impacts
  • 6.) Respect Wildlife
  • 7.) Be Considerate of Other Visitors

Following these principles is easy, and important to maintaining the wilderness we love. Just as you wouldn’t want a guest to destroy your home, you shouldn’t destroy the habitat of animals and plants outside. Leaving behind trash is a serious issue that is commonly produced by hikers, endangering the lives of animals which can easily mistake it for food, on top of directly polluting the environment. However, not all environmental issues are as obvious or as simple to prevent as littering.

For example, the second principle, “Travel and Camp on Durable Surfaces,” asks outdoor enthusiasts to consider the physical effects of travel on the grounds around them. At the most basic level, this calls for visitors to use common sense and avoid moving through areas with undeveloped pathways or delicate plant life. Research shows [3] that movement itself through a natural environment, either on foot or on horseback, can reduce the number of plant species living around the traffic. It is important to consider this when exploring new areas, as frequent traffic through an otherwise untouched natural area can affect its ecosystem negatively. Similarly, camping should be done at established campsites, or locations with minimal fauna and potential for runoff into nearby waterways. Even conscientious hikers and campers can easily miss these points, as the impacts from trampling and other habitat interference typically occurs gradually.

Building a strong knowledge on what is detrimental to the wildlife around you can be very helpful to minimizing your impact while outdoors. We all care for the wilderness, so please consider the principles listed above when you are out there. Following them can make the difference between another generation enjoying the beauty of a natural formation and it simply being gone.
