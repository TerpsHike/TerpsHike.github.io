Keep it Cool

Nothing should upset a hiker more than the unnecessary burning of the forests. You love nature, we love nature, and all of us should be doing everything we can to keep it safe. Wildfires are a threat we should all work together to solve.

Fighting Forest Fires

Wildfires are surprisingly common in Maryland. Although not as prone as places like California, our state experiences over 300 wildfires every year, burning down almost four thousand acres of forest. While wildfires can occur naturally and in some cases can increase the health of forest, this is the exception rather than the rule. In 2015, only four percent of the the state's forest fires were caused by lightning strikes. The remaining 96% were the result of human carelessness.

As a hiker, you will be spending more time in the woods than most people. This means that you are more likely to cause a wildfire accidentally, and therefore have a responsibility to be educated and careful. The number one cause of wildfires is the improper extinguishing of things like campfires and stoves, or the burning of other materials in the forest. Make sure that you extinguish your fires and turn off your camps stoves! Even after you poor sand or water on the fire, there is a good chance that the embers underneath are hot and could reignite after you leave. It's important to stir the fire looking for red coals. Additionally, when you start a fire make sure the embers are rising away from trees. Don’t make your fire directly underneath branches, as a dead or dry tree can be set alight.

The other leading causes of wildfires are smoking, downed power lines, discarded ashes, fireworks, and railroad issues. These are things to keep an eye out for, because if you see something of this nature it's important that you report it as soon as possible. Don’t try to fight even a small fire yourself, as they can spread very quickly and you probably won't have the right equipment. The best people to notify of a fire or potential fire is the Maryland Fire Crew, a forest ranger, or fire tower staff, but simply calling 911 and reporting the issue to a local fire station is sufficient.
